# Maintenance Policy

- Status: company policy
- Owner: Founder or explicitly assigned service owner
- Scope: continuing responsibility after delivery

## Purpose

Maintenance keeps an agreed system secure, current, understandable, supported,
or improving within explicit boundaries. It is not an implied warranty,
unlimited support, or a substitute for client ownership.
The constitutional basis is the
[Company Bible](../COMPANY_BIBLE.md) (*I.III.5–6; I.IV.5; I.XVI.1–3*).

## Principles

- Maintenance is agreed, scoped, owned, and reviewable.
- Responsibility follows documented system and information boundaries.
- Preventive work is preferred to repeated emergency work.
- Security, dependencies, recovery, and documentation evolve together.
- Availability, response, and resolution commitments are never implied.
- The client retains an understandable exit and handover path.

## Adaptable Levels

### Essential Care

Bounded maintenance and essential continuity for a stable system.

An engagement may include agreed dependency review, critical updates, basic
operational review, documentation correction, backup or recovery verification,
and issue triage. Exact responsibility and cadence are defined in the proposal.

### Growth Care

Maintenance plus planned improvement for a system that is evolving.

An engagement may add periodic technical review, prioritised improvement,
performance or accessibility work, small approved changes, and roadmap
alignment. It does not create an unlimited development allocation.

### Continuous Operations

Broader operational continuity and active technical stewardship for a system
whose operation requires continuing attention.

An engagement may include more frequent review, observability, incident
coordination, recovery readiness, controlled release support, capacity
consideration, and planned evolution. It does not imply continuous staffing or
availability unless separately agreed.

These levels are adaptable responsibility models, not rigid packages. Names do
not establish price, frequency, service level, or included technology.

## Required Maintenance Schedule

Each engagement records:

| Area | Required decision |
| --- | --- |
| Systems | Exact in-scope systems and environments |
| Responsibility | Client, YSWORKS, and third-party ownership |
| Activities | Preventive, corrective, review, and change boundaries |
| Cadence | Planned review and maintenance windows |
| Support | Approved channel, priority model, and availability |
| Security | Updates, access review, vulnerabilities, and incident boundary |
| Continuity | Backup, restore, rollback, and recovery responsibilities |
| Observability | Signals reviewed and information safe for the client |
| Changes | Approval, validation, release, and documentation method |
| Exclusions | Out-of-scope systems, work, and third-party obligations |
| Commercial | Billing, expenses, usage, renewal, and termination |
| Review | Service owner, review date, and change trigger |

## Entry

Maintenance begins only after a supported baseline is identified, material
risks and technical debt are disclosed, access is approved, responsibilities are
accepted, recovery expectations are understood, and commercial terms are in
force.

YSWORKS may require a stabilisation engagement before accepting responsibility
for an unknown or unsupported system.

## Changes

Maintenance does not bypass change control. Production releases, infrastructure
changes, access changes, and other privileged actions require their applicable
authorisation, validation, audit, and rollback path.

## Review And Exit

Review the service when scope, risk, usage, dependencies, ownership, regulation,
or business criticality changes. On exit, revoke access, return or delete
information correctly, provide the agreed records, identify unsupported areas,
and confirm the final responsibility boundary.
