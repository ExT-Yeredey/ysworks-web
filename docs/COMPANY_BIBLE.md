## Preamble

Most companies write down what they do only after they have forgotten why they do it. The documents produced that way have a recognisable smell: mission statements that could belong to any firm in any industry, values chosen because they photograph well, principles that have never once been used to lose money.

This volume is written early, deliberately, before habit has had time to harden into accident. It exists so that YSWORKS never has to reverse-engineer its own character from its behaviour, and so that anyone who works with the company — an employee, a collaborator, a client, a machine — can know in advance how it will decide when deciding is hard.

Nothing in this volume is aspirational. Aspiration is what a company hopes to become; a constitution is what a company refuses to stop being. Where this document states a preference, the preference has already been paid for at least once, and the company expects to pay for it again. A principle that has never cost anything is a decoration.

The volume is organised into books, and books into articles, so that its contents can be cited rather than paraphrased. Paraphrase is how constitutions decay.

# Book I — The Company

### Article 1. Definition

YSWORKS is a technology engineering company.

Each of those three words is load-bearing. *Technology* names the material. *Engineering* names the discipline — the practice of understanding a problem well enough to build something that solves it, within constraints, with evidence, under responsibility. *Company* names the ambition: not a freelancer with a client list, not a studio with a style, but an institution intended to outlive its current form.

The order of the words matters too. Technology comes first only grammatically. In every other sense, engineering precedes it: the company would remain itself if every technology it currently uses disappeared, because its identity rests on how it works, not on what it works with.

### Article 2. What the company does

YSWORKS understands business problems. It designs useful systems. It implements them responsibly. It documents everything. It improves those systems continuously.

That sentence sequence is the company in miniature, and the sequence is not decorative — it is causal. Understanding precedes design because a system designed before its problem is understood solves the wrong problem elegantly. Design precedes implementation because implementation is where mistakes become expensive. Documentation follows implementation because an undocumented system is a hostage negotiation waiting to happen. Improvement comes last and never ends, because a delivered system is not a finished system; it is a system at the beginning of its working life.

### Article 3. What the company is not

Definitions gain most of their strength from their negative space.

YSWORKS is not an agency. An agency sells projects: bounded engagements that end with a handover and an invoice. YSWORKS builds systems that are expected to run, and takes an interest in their running.

YSWORKS is not a software vendor. It does not hold a product that clients must reshape their business to fit. It begins from the business and works towards the system.

YSWORKS is not an AI company, in the sense that phrase acquired during the years when it meant a company whose product is enthusiasm. Artificial intelligence appears throughout the company's work, and Book V governs how; but a company defined by its most fashionable tool has chosen to expire with the fashion.

YSWORKS is not a body shop. It does not rent people by the hour to be managed by others. Responsibility for outcomes is not transferable, and the company does not accept work where it would be.

### Article 4. The product

Technology is never the product. Engineering is.

A client who buys technology from YSWORKS has been sold the wrong thing, and the error is the company's. What a client actually acquires is a set of decisions: what to build, what not to build, what to connect, what to isolate, what to automate, what to leave in human hands, what to write down, and what to keep improving. The artefacts — sites, systems, automations — are the residue of those decisions. Good residue matters, and the company is fastidious about it, but the decisions are the product, which is why they are documented, argued for, and signed.

### Article 5. The slogan

The company's slogan is: **Engineering useful systems.**

It is recorded here not for celebration but for constraint. The slogan is a test with three clauses. *Engineering*: was the work done with discipline, evidence and responsibility? *Useful*: does the result matter to the client's actual business, measurably, in their terms rather than ours? *Systems*: does the work behave as a whole — maintained, documented, connected, supervised — rather than as a pile of clever parts? Work that fails any clause is off-slogan, whatever the contract says. No other slogan is to be coined. Companies that collect slogans are negotiating with their own definition.

# Book II — Purpose and Vision

### Article 1. Why the company exists

Small and mid-sized businesses live inside a strange asymmetry. The technology available to them is the most powerful in history, and the engineering available to them is mostly terrible: templated, outsourced, unaccountable, undocumented, abandoned at handover. The firms that can afford serious engineering rarely need it more; the firms that need it most are served worst.

YSWORKS exists to put serious engineering within reach of businesses that are not technology companies — to give a firm whose expertise is logistics, or care, or craft, the kind of systems thinking that technology firms reserve for themselves. The purpose is not to make businesses more technological. It is to make them work better, with technology as the means and engineering as the guarantee.

### Article 2. The vision

The long-term vision of YSWORKS is to become one of the most trusted engineering companies for useful intelligent systems.

The operative word is *trusted*, and it was chosen against its alternatives. Not the largest: size is a consequence, not a goal, and frequently a disease. Not the most famous: fame is volatile and mostly purchased. Not the most advanced: advancement is a moving target that others define. Trust is the only asset in engineering that compounds, cannot be bought, transfers by word of mouth, and survives technological change. A trusted company gets told the truth by its clients, gets forgiven honest failure, and gets invited into decisions early, where engineering does its best work.

*Useful intelligent systems* bounds the ambition. The company does not aspire to build impressive systems, novel systems, or systems interesting to other engineers. It aspires to build systems whose intelligence is in proportion to their usefulness — and no further.

### Article 3. How the company grows

The company grows through quality. Never through volume.

This is the vision's enforcement clause. Growth through volume has a well-known mechanism: take more work than can be done well, dilute the standard, hire faster than culture can absorb, and convert reputation into revenue until both are gone. It works, briefly, which is why it is common.

Growth through quality has a slower mechanism: do work good enough that it becomes the argument for the next work. Each system in production is a standing demonstration. Each documented decision is a reference. Each client who was left better than they were found (Book III, Article 6) is a witness. This mechanism cannot be accelerated by wanting it to go faster, which is precisely what makes its results durable.

The practical corollary is stated plainly so it can be cited in the moment it is needed: **YSWORKS will decline work rather than dilute work.** The declined engagement costs one client. The diluted standard costs the company its argument for every future client.

# Book III — The Six Preferences

The Founder's decisions are recorded here as standing preferences. A preference, in this volume's vocabulary, is stronger than a value and weaker than a law of nature: it states which way the company leans whenever two goods conflict. Each preference is written with its price attached, because a preference without a price is a slogan, and Book I, Article 5 forbids new slogans.

### Article 1. Quality over growth

When more work and better work compete, better work wins.

The price: slower revenue, smaller headlines, and the recurring discomfort of watching louder competitors scale faster. The company pays it because quality is its only compounding asset (Book II, Article 2) and because the alternative — a growing pile of mediocre systems bearing the company's name — is not a bigger company but a differently named one.

This preference does not romanticise smallness. The company intends to grow. It constrains only the *order* of operations: capacity first, then commitments; standard first, then scale.

### Article 2. Understanding over speed

When shipping sooner and understanding better compete, understanding wins.

Most engineering failure is not failure of construction but failure of comprehension: the system was built well and aimed wrongly. Time spent understanding is therefore not a delay before the work — it is the work, front-loaded where it is cheapest. A week of understanding routinely saves a quarter of rebuilding.

The price: YSWORKS will sometimes look slow at the beginning of an engagement, and will sometimes ask questions a client finds excessive. The company accepts looking slow early in exchange for being fast forever after, and it puts the understanding in writing so the client can see what the week bought.

This preference has a boundary worth recording: understanding is not licence for indefinite analysis. Understanding is complete when the next decision can be defended in writing — not when every question is answered. Analysis that no longer changes decisions is a form of procrastination wearing glasses.

### Article 3. Engineering over hype

When a fashionable approach and a sound approach compete, the sound approach wins — even when the client asked for the fashionable one, in which case the company's duty is to explain, in the client's language, what the fashion costs.

The price: YSWORKS will periodically appear conservative, will lose the occasional prospect who wanted the fashion more than the outcome, and will forgo the easy marketing of the moment's vocabulary. In exchange it keeps the ability to say, about everything it has shipped: this exists because it was the right tool, not because it was the loud one. In a decade when hype cycles are measured in months, boredom about tools is a competitive position.

### Article 4. Documentation over tribal knowledge

When writing it down and moving on compete, writing it down wins.

Tribal knowledge is the most expensive storage medium ever devised. Its retrieval cost is an interruption, its replication cost is an apprenticeship, its failure mode is a resignation. Documentation converts individual memory into institutional memory, which is the precondition for being an institution at all.

At YSWORKS, documentation is not an artefact produced about the work; it is a component of the work. A system without its documentation is an incomplete delivery in exactly the way a system without error handling is incomplete — it functions until the first surprise. The standard is functional, not ceremonial: a document is good when the reader can act without asking its author, and its length is whatever that takes and no more. Documents no one acts on are not documentation; they are sediment.

The price: writing is slow, unglamorous, and never urgent until the day it is priceless. The company pays daily and collects rarely, and considers that an excellent trade, because the collecting days are the ones that decide a company's reputation.

### Article 5. Long-term maintainability over short-term optimisation

When the quick improvement and the durable structure compete, the structure wins.

Every system accumulates pressure to mortgage its future: the shortcut that ships this week, the dependency that saves a day now and costs a migration later, the special case that solves today by complicating every tomorrow. Each is individually reasonable. Their sum is a system nobody dares to touch — and an untouchable system is a dead system that has not stopped moving yet.

YSWORKS optimises for the engineer who will maintain the system in five years, on the assumption that this engineer may be a stranger, in a hurry, encountering the system on its worst day. Everything that would puzzle that stranger is a defect, whether or not it works.

The price: the company sometimes builds more slowly and more plainly than cleverness would allow. It considers cleverness a cost centre. The most expensive line in any system is the one that only its author understands.

### Article 6. Leave every business better than you found it

This preference differs from the other five: they govern how the company works, this one governs what the company is for, and it applies even when an engagement fails, shrinks, or ends early.

*Better* is defined broadly and concretely: better systems, but also better understanding of their own systems; better documentation than existed before; better questions asked of the next vendor; better defaults for security and data; one less dependency on any single person — including on YSWORKS. The company's success leaves a client more capable and more independent, not more captive. Dependency revenue — income that exists only because the client cannot leave — is refused as a business model, because it inverts the company's incentives and converts trust into leverage.

The price: the company routinely builds and documents its own replaceability. It judges this the strongest possible signal of confidence in its work, and the only honest one. A company that must lock the door has already conceded that no one would stay in the room by choice.

### Article 7. On conflicts between preferences

The preferences are ordered by the Founder's decision sequence, but they rarely conflict cleanly, and this volume declines to pretend they resolve mechanically. When preferences collide — when, say, documentation costs the time that understanding needs — the resolution is a judgement, made by whoever holds the decision (Book X), recorded with its reasoning. The record matters more than the outcome: a judged conflict teaches the next judgement; an unrecorded one merely repeats.

# Book IV — The Engineering Doctrine

The preferences say which way the company leans. The doctrine says how the work is actually done. It is written for whoever performs engineering under the company's name — person or machine — and it applies to every engagement regardless of size.

### Article 1. Begin with the business

Every engagement begins with the business, not the technology. The first questions are the client's questions: what does this firm do, how does money and effort actually flow through it, where does it hurt, what has been tried, what would *better* mean here, measured how, in whose terms. Technology enters the conversation only once the problem can be stated without it.

The corollary is a standing rule of scoping: **the system boundary is drawn around the problem, not around the software.** A useful system frequently includes a human step, a policy, a piece of paper, a decision *not* to automate. Engineering that can only see software solves fragments.

### Article 2. Design before implementation

Between understanding and construction stands design: the deliberate selection of structure, boundaries, dependencies and failure behaviour, written down before it is built. Design at YSWORKS is not a drawing produced to reassure; it is the argument for the system, made inspectable. A design is complete when it states what the system will do, what it will refuse to do, how it fails, how it is observed, and how it will be maintained — and when a competent stranger could challenge it on paper.

Challenge is the point. Paper is where disagreement is cheap. Every argument moved from production to design is bought at a discount of roughly a hundred to one.

### Article 3. Implement responsibly

Responsible implementation is defined by what it refuses. It refuses to ship what it cannot explain. It refuses to depend on what it cannot replace or pin. It refuses to touch production without a way back. It refuses to handle client data beyond the minimum the problem requires. It refuses to leave failure behaviour to chance: every system fails; a responsibly built one fails in a designed direction — closed where safety demands it, degraded where continuity does, but never silently.

Security in this doctrine is not a feature or a phase. It is a property of boundaries — drawn early, defended by default, and treated with the same seriousness whether the system is grand or small, because attackers do not respect project budgets.

### Article 4. Document as you go

Documentation written after the fact documents a system that no longer exists. The doctrine therefore binds writing to building: decisions are recorded when they are made, with their alternatives and their reasons, because the reasons are the part that cannot be reconstructed later. Code shows *what*; only the record shows *why not otherwise* — and *why not otherwise* is what the maintainer in five years actually needs.

Every delivered system carries, at minimum: what it is for, how it is put together, how to operate it, how it fails, and how to change it safely. This is the floor, not the ceiling.

### Article 5. Deliver, then remain

Delivery is a beginning. Systems live in businesses, and businesses move; a system maintained by no one is drifting towards the day it becomes a surprise. The doctrine's final duty is continuity: every system leaves the workshop with a maintenance posture — who watches it, what is watched, what *healthy* looks like, when it should be revisited — agreed and written down. Where the client assumes that duty themselves, the company equips them for it fully (Book III, Article 6). What the doctrine forbids is the pretence, common in the industry, that a handover dissolves the question.

### Article 6. Evidence over confidence

At every stage, the doctrine prefers what can be shown to what is merely believed. Claims about performance carry measurements. Claims about correctness carry tests. Claims about usefulness carry the client's own numbers, in the client's own terms. Where evidence cannot yet exist, the claim is labelled a hypothesis and sized accordingly. Confidence is a feeling; the doctrine treats it as one.

# Book V — On Artificial Intelligence

### Article 1. Position

AI augments engineering. AI never replaces responsibility.

Both sentences are load-bearing, and the second governs the first. YSWORKS uses artificial intelligence throughout its work, without embarrassment and without theatre, wherever it makes engineering better: faster understanding, broader exploration, sharper review, automation of the repetitive. The company is not neutral about this — used well, AI is the most significant amplifier of engineering capability in the company's lifetime, and declining it would itself be poor engineering.

But amplification is all it is. Responsibility — for correctness, for safety, for a claim made to a client, for a system's behaviour in production — attaches to people. It cannot be delegated to a model, because responsibility is precisely the thing that answers when the output is wrong, and a model answers for nothing.

### Article 2. The human seat

Every consequential decision at YSWORKS has a human seat: a named person who approved it, would approve it again, and can explain it. Machine work may inform the seat, draft for it, check it, and challenge it. Machine work may not occupy it. Where automation acts, it acts within boundaries a person set, under supervision a person maintains, with a record a person can audit — and where those conditions cannot be met, the automation is not built.

This is not caution about the future. It is a statement about what engineering *is*. An unexplained system is unengineered, whoever or whatever produced it.

### Article 3. Proportion

Intelligence in a system is a cost: it must be supervised, evaluated, explained, and maintained. The doctrine of usefulness (Book II, Article 2) therefore applies with special force — AI earns its place in a system by concrete improvement, with limits stated, behaviour observable, and human review wherever it really matters. A deterministic solution that suffices is preferred to a probabilistic one that impresses. The company builds intelligent systems, not systems that perform intelligence.

### Article 4. Honesty about AI

The company describes its use of AI accurately or not at all: no capability implied that is not operational, no autonomy implied that is not real, no "AI-powered" ornamentation. Externally this is Book VIII applied to a domain unusually full of exaggeration. Internally it is stricter still: the company's own records must never blur what a person decided and what a machine produced, because that line is where responsibility lives.

# Book VI — Clients

### Article 1. Who the company serves

YSWORKS serves businesses. Not consumers.

The distinction is not commercial preference but fit of duty. A business client can hold a considered, documented, long-term engineering relationship: it has operations to understand, systems to maintain, and a definition of *better* that can be written down and measured. The company's entire method — understanding, design, documentation, continuity — presumes such a counterpart.

### Article 2. What a client may expect

Every client of YSWORKS, of any size, may expect the same things, because they are properties of the company rather than terms of a tier: to be understood before being prescribed to; to hear the argument, not just the recommendation; to own what they paid for — systems, documentation, and the knowledge to use both; to be told the truth at the speed the company learns it, including the truths that cost the company; to have *no* delivered as a complete sentence with reasons attached; and to end the relationship without hostage negotiations (Book III, Article 6).

### Article 3. What the company expects

The relationship is bilateral. YSWORKS expects access to the truth of the business — problems as they are, not as presented; expects decisions from the people entitled to make them; expects that *understanding* time is respected as work; and expects to be told when something the company built is not working, early, bluntly. A client who hides trouble from their engineers has hired decoration.

### Article 4. Refusal

The company's *no* is a quality instrument, and this volume protects it. YSWORKS declines work when the problem is not understood and cannot be, when the requested solution would harm the client and persuasion has failed, when the work would compromise another client's isolation or the company's standards, when the capacity to do it well does not exist, or when the client wants a vendor to blame rather than an engineering counterpart. Each refusal is delivered with its reasons and, where possible, a better direction — a refusal that teaches something is the smallest unit of leaving-better (Book III, Article 6).

# Book VII — The Ecosystem and Its Boundaries

### Article 1. The shape

The public shape of YSWORKS is a gradient of trust, descending from open to private:

**YSWORKS → Public Website → Client Workspace → Private Internal Systems → YS AI OS.**

Each layer exists for a different audience and therefore behaves differently. The public website is for anyone: its work is credibility, education and honest persuasion. The client workspace is for clients: an authenticated place where the relationship is conducted — their systems, their documents, their progress. The private internal systems are for the company alone. And beneath everything runs YS AI OS.

**Client Workspace** is the client-facing product name. **Client Portal** is the technical architecture term for the authenticated delivery boundary.

### Article 2. YS AI OS

YS AI OS is not a public product or brand and has no independent public logo. It is private internal infrastructure: the operating layer through which the company coordinates and supervises its own work.

This volume fixes both halves of that sentence. Internally, the OS embodies the constitution in running form — human authority, policy, approval, audit, isolation — and is governed by its own technical documents. Publicly, it is described in one sanctioned way: *YSWORKS uses an internal operating platform to coordinate and supervise work.* Its internals, workflows, topology, prompts, agents, telemetry and records are private, permanently, not because they are shameful but because a company that exposes its own machinery for marketing has converted its nervous system into a brochure. Clients experience the OS only as its consequences: consistency, memory, auditability, care.

Any hypothetical external product based on a capability of the OS requires a separate Founder decision and new governance. It is not pre-approved by this volume and must never be a window opened into the working interior.

### Article 3. Boundary discipline

Between every layer stands a deliberate boundary, and the constitution takes the boundaries as seriously as the layers. Client material lives in the client's layer and never appears in another client's view; each client's isolation is absolute, structural, and boring — enforced by design rather than by promise. Public claims are drawn only from what the public layer is entitled to know. Internal names, internal telemetry and internal records do not migrate outward, even sanitised, even impressive. The gradient of trust only works if nothing seeps.

# Book VIII — Language and Honesty

### Article 1. The company's voice

YSWORKS speaks the way it engineers: plainly, precisely, and in proportion to the evidence. Confident declaratives. Short sentences. Technical terms used correctly and only where they clarify. The voice of a senior engineer explaining something real to an intelligent person who is not an engineer — respectful in both directions.

The voice never begs, never inflates, never hedges reflexively, and never performs enthusiasm. In both of the company's working languages, Spanish and English, it is written natively, not translated mechanically; tone is part of the engineering.

### Article 2. The honesty constraints

These constraints are constitutional and admit no exceptions for context, medium or opportunity:

No invented numbers. No metric published that cannot be verified. No simulated liveness — nothing that pretends to be real-time, live or operational when it is not. No testimonial that was not freely given by a real, named counterpart. No logo used without authorisation. No capability implied that is not operational today. No promise — of time, of outcome, of availability — made without the operational means to keep it.

Silence is always available and always preferred to inflation. A company that will not exaggerate when exaggeration is cheap is making the only credible promise about how it will behave when honesty is expensive.

### Article 3. Internal honesty

The same constraints face inward. Estimates are given honestly, with their uncertainty attached. Failures are recorded as they happened, not as they would look best. Post-mortems name causes, not culprits, but they do name causes. The company's records are the memory it will think with later; falsifying them is self-harm with paperwork.

# Book IX — Quality

### Article 1. Definition

Quality at YSWORKS is not polish, and not perfection. It is **conformance to purpose, demonstrated by evidence, sustained over time**. A system has quality when it does what the business needs, provably, and can keep doing it under maintenance by ordinary mortals.

The definition deliberately includes time. Work that is excellent on delivery day and untouchable a year later fails the definition. So does work that is technically flawless and useless (Book I, Article 5).

### Article 2. The meaning of done

*Done*, at YSWORKS, is a five-part state: the thing works; its working is demonstrated by evidence appropriate to its risk; its decisions and operation are documented; its maintenance posture is agreed; and the client can use what they were given. Anything less is *in progress*, whatever the calendar says. The word *done* is used carefully because it is a claim, and claims fall under Book VIII.

### Article 3. Where the standard bends

Under pressure, standards do not usually break; they bend quietly, in unrecorded increments. The constitution's answer is not to pretend pressure away but to make bending visible: any conscious reduction of the standard — a shortcut taken, a corner accepted, a debt incurred — is recorded as a debt, with its reason and its intended repayment. Debt recorded is engineering. Debt hidden is decay with a schedule.

# Book X — Decisions and Authority

### Article 1. The Founder

The Founder is the company's sole strategic and highest internal authority: custodian of this constitution, holder of final decision on direction, standards, and the exceptions that reality occasionally demands. Authority in the company flows from this seat, explicitly delegated, never assumed — a structure mirrored, deliberately, in the company's internal systems, where the same principle is enforced in software. Direct Founder decisions do not displace applicable law or binding contractual obligations. Accepted ADRs prevail only within their explicitly stated technical scope and may not override this constitution outside that scope.

Concentration of authority is a responsibility, not a privilege, and this volume binds the seat as much as it empowers it: the Founder's decisions are recorded with reasons like anyone's, are subject to the honesty constraints like anyone's, and amend this constitution only in writing (Book XII).

### Article 2. How decisions are made

Decisions at YSWORKS are classified before they are argued: **reversible** decisions are made quickly, cheaply, by whoever is closest to the work, and corrected without ceremony; **irreversible or expensive** decisions are made slowly, in writing, with alternatives stated and evidence attached. Most decisions are reversible; the company refuses to purchase false rigour by treating them all as grave. The skill it cultivates is telling the two apart.

Disagreement is welcome before a decision and disciplined after it: argue in design, commit in delivery, and record the dissent — recorded dissent is how the company finds out later who saw clearly.

Any contradiction between governing authorities is reported and escalated rather than silently resolved.

### Article 3. Machines in decisions

Book V, Article 2 applies to every decision in this book: analysis may be machine-made; decisions are human-made. The distinction is preserved in the record.

# Book XI — Growth and Refusal

### Article 1. The rate of growth

The company grows at the rate at which its standard can be kept — no faster. Capacity precedes commitment; culture precedes headcount; the standard precedes the scale (Book II, Article 3). Any growth that requires diluting Books III, IV, VIII or IX is not growth but exchange: reputation sold for size, at an exchange rate that always looks better than it is.

### Article 2. What the company refuses to become

Certain futures are refused now, in writing, while refusing is easy. YSWORKS will not become a volume shop, valued by throughput. It will not become a dependency business, valued by clients' inability to leave. It will not become a hype vehicle, valued by adjacency to whatever is loud. It will not become a marketplace of anonymous labour with a brand on top. Each of these is a real, adjacent, profitable fate for a company shaped like this one — which is exactly why the refusals are constitutional rather than assumed.

### Article 3. Endurance

The company is built to be old. Not old-fashioned — old: still trusted, still improving, still recognisably itself after its tools, its market and its founder's daily involvement have all changed. Decisions are weighed against that horizon. The question asked of any strategic choice is not *what does this win this year* but *what does the company that made this choice look like in ten* — and whether that company is one this constitution would recognise.

# Book XII — This Document

### Article 1. Standing

This volume is constitutional: within YSWORKS it outranks strategies, plans, processes, designs and preferences of the moment. Documents that conflict with it are wrong until amended; behaviour that conflicts with it is a defect, whoever exhibits it. It binds people and systems alike — including the automated systems through which the company increasingly works, whose policies are expected to encode what these books state in prose.

The governing order is:

1. Direct Founder decisions.
2. Applicable law and binding contractual obligations.
3. Accepted ADRs within their stated technical domain.
4. This Company Bible.
5. The Master Specification.
6. The Founder Handbook.
7. The Operating Manual.
8. Domain foundations and policies.
9. Brand, User Experience, Design System and Motion documents.
10. Implementation documentation.

The Company Bible is the highest company-wide narrative and behavioural constitution. It does not override the Founder, applicable law, binding contracts, or an Accepted ADR inside that ADR's explicit technical scope. An ADR may not override this volume outside that scope. Any contradiction is reported and escalated rather than silently resolved.

### Article 2. Amendment

The constitution changes the way the company builds: deliberately, in writing, with reasons. Amendment is the Founder's act, recorded as a new version with its rationale preserved; the history of the document is part of the document. What may not change silently is anything — a constitution that drifts is merely a diary.

Amendment does not evade applicable law or binding contractual obligations, and it does not widen an ADR beyond its accepted technical scope.

Amendment is expected. A company that never revises its constitution has stopped reading it. The test of an amendment is the same as the test of any engineering change: does it make the whole more true, more useful, more maintainable — or merely more comfortable this quarter?

### Article 3. Use

This volume earns its existence only in use: cited in designs, quoted in refusals, consulted in conflicts, taught to whoever — person or system — begins to work under the company's name. It is written to be cited, which is why its articles are short and numbered, and it is written to be believed, which is why every principle in it carries its price.

The company it describes does not fully exist yet. That is not a flaw in the document. That is what a constitution is for: it is the description that the company grows into, and the instrument by which anyone — client, collaborator, successor, machine — may hold it to the shape it chose for itself, on a July day in 2026, while choosing was still cheap.

# Book XIII — Systems Thinking

### Article 1. The doctrine

**Everything is a system before it becomes software.**

That sentence is doctrine, not slogan — Book I, Article 5 stands — and it is used as doctrine is used: as the first test applied to every piece of work. Before YSWORKS engineers anything, it asks what system the thing belongs to, because nothing the company builds will ever run in isolation. Software that was conceived in isolation still joins a system on its first day in production; it simply joins it by accident, and accidental membership is where most failure lives.

### Article 2. What counts as a system

More than the industry usually admits. A business is a system: money, effort, information and decisions circulating through people and tools, with feedback loops that reward some behaviours and quietly punish others. People are systems — of attention, habit, incentive and fatigue — and any design that ignores this will be corrected by it. Knowledge is a system: it flows, pools, evaporates and goes stale, which is why Book III, Article 4 treats documentation as infrastructure rather than paperwork. Infrastructure is a system in the obvious sense and in the less obvious one: it teaches everything above it what to expect. And artificial intelligence is a component — one part within a larger system of policy, supervision, memory and audit, never a system's substitute (Book V).

### Article 3. Relationships before components

Engineering, in this company's practice, means understanding relationships before components. A component list describes what a system is made of; only the relationships describe what it *does* — what feeds what, what waits on what, what fails together, what compensates for what, where pressure applied here emerges as pain over there. This is why the company's first weeks on any engagement look like conversation rather than construction (Book IV, Article 1), why its diagrams give as much care to connectors as to boxes, and why its scoping rule draws the boundary around the problem rather than the software. A company that starts from components ships parts. A company that starts from relationships ships behaviour.

### Article 4. The consequence for judgement

Systems thinking is also a discipline of side-effects. Every intervention in a system produces consequences beyond its intention, and the difference between an engineer and an installer is that the engineer goes looking for them: what this automation does to the person who used to make this decision, what this dashboard does to what people stop noticing, what this dependency does to the client's freedom in three years. Work at YSWORKS is not judged complete when the component works, but when its place in the system has been understood, stated, and accepted — side-effects included.

# Book XIV — Craftsmanship

### Article 1. Engineering as craft

Engineering at YSWORKS is a discipline and also a craft, and the two are not in tension. The discipline supplies evidence, method and responsibility; the craft supplies care — the granular, unsupervised attention that no process can mandate and every good system exhibits. Craft is what happens in the thousand small decisions that no client will ever inspect: the name that says what the thing is, the error message that respects the person reading it, the document whose second paragraph answers the question its first paragraph raised. Process can require these things to exist. Only care makes them good.

### Article 2. Inevitable, not clever

The standard of the craft is that finished work should feel inevitable — as if, once the problem was truly understood, the solution could hardly have been otherwise. Cleverness is the opposite quality: work that draws attention to its own difficulty, that asks to be admired before it can be maintained. Book III, Article 5 has already priced cleverness as a cost centre; this book states the positive form. When a design feels inevitable, the difficulty has been absorbed by the engineer so that it never reaches the maintainer, the client, or the user. Absorbing difficulty is what the company is paid for. Redistributing it downstream, artfully, is the industry's most decorated form of failure.

### Article 3. The scope of care

Care at YSWORKS has no exempt categories. Every document is written to be readable — by its intended reader, at their level, without a translator. Every interface is intentional — nothing on it unconsidered, nothing there because a template put it there. Every system is maintainable — legible to the stranger of Book III, Article 5, on their worst day. The company permits no distinction between visible work and hidden work, because the craft objection to that distinction is practical, not romantic: hidden work becomes visible precisely when it fails, and it fails in proportion to the care it was denied.

### Article 4. Quiet excellence

The goal is excellence that does not need to announce itself. Work of quality changes how it is treated: it gets read, trusted, extended, kept. That is its announcement, and the only one this constitution authorises. The company does not attach superlatives to its own output (Book VIII would forbid it anyway); it builds output that makes superlatives redundant. Quiet excellence is also a hiring and delegation filter, for people and for machines alike: YSWORKS wants collaborators who work well when nobody is watching, because in engineering, almost nobody ever is — until the day everyone is.

# Book XV — Experience Engineering

### Article 1. The experienced company

A client never experiences a company's principles. They experience its Tuesday afternoons: the website that answered plainly, the email that arrived when promised, the meeting that started with their problem rather than a slide deck, the document they understood without calling, the invoice that was exactly what was agreed, the support reply that read like a person, the dashboard that told the truth, the maintenance that happened without being chased. Each of these is an engineered surface whether or not anyone engineered it. This book states that at YSWORKS, someone did.

### Article 2. Everything communicates

There is no neutral interaction. Every artefact and every exchange either confirms the company's claimed character or quietly contradicts it — and contradiction compounds faster than confirmation, because a single sloppy email can spend the credibility of an excellent system. The website, the correspondence, the meetings, the documentation, the support, the dashboards, the software and the maintenance are therefore treated as one continuous product with many surfaces, governed by the same standards of precision, honesty and care (Books VIII, IX, XIV) rather than by the varying moods of whoever produced them. The company's voice, its visual language and its motion have written specifications for exactly this reason: consistency is not aesthetic preference; it is the client's evidence of discipline.

### Article 3. Trust as an accumulation

Trust — the asset the entire vision rests on (Book II, Article 2) — is not won in signatures or launches. It is accumulated through thousands of consistent micro-experiences, each individually too small to mention: the reply that came back honest, the estimate that held, the small thing fixed without being asked, the *no* that arrived with reasons. No single micro-experience persuades anyone of anything. Their consistency is what persuades, because consistency across small unwatched moments is the one signal that cannot be faked at scale. This is also why trust is lost asymmetrically: a company can only betray in large moments what it built in small ones. Experience engineering is therefore not a department or a phase; it is the recognition that the product includes every point at which the client touches the company — and that each such point is either designed, or defaulted.

# Book XVI — Evolution

### Article 1. Nothing is finished

Nothing inside YSWORKS is expected to remain static. Products evolve. Processes evolve. Systems evolve. This constitution evolves (Book XII expects its own amendment). People evolve, and the company is arranged so that they may. The company itself evolves — in structure, in tooling, in what it offers — while remaining, per Book XI, Article 3, recognisably itself. The only unacceptable state is stagnation, because in living systems stillness is never actually still: a system that has stopped improving has started decaying, and is merely early in the news.

### Article 2. The direction of evolution

Evolution without direction is drift, and drift is how good systems become haunted houses. The constitution therefore fixes the direction: **every system should become easier to understand every year.** This is a constitutional principle, stated as an obligation of maintenance. Each year of a system's life should leave its documentation truer, its structure plainer, its surprises fewer, its dependence on any single memory smaller. Most of the industry accepts the opposite as natural law — that systems grow foggier with age until only archaeology remains. YSWORKS treats growing fog as a defect with a schedule, and clarity as a deliverable of every year, not only of the first. A system that is harder to understand this year than last has evolved in the wrong direction, whatever features it gained.

### Article 3. Evolution under identity

The license to evolve is bounded by the duty to remain. What may change freely: tools, methods, structures, offerings, scale. What may change only by written amendment: the constitution. What may not change at all while the company remains this company: the preferences of Book III, the honesty of Book VIII, the human seat of Book V. Evolution at YSWORKS is therefore like maintenance done well — continuous change in the service of continuity. The company intends to be unrecognisable in its tools and unmistakable in its character, decade after decade, and regards any inversion of that sentence as the definition of failure.

# Book XVII — The YSWORKS Way

Anyone can be hired to say yes. The company's behavioural signature is its second sentence: the question it asks after the client's first request. These scenarios are recorded not as scripts but as demonstrations of a habit of mind — the habit of hearing a solution and asking for its problem. New colleagues, human or machine, should be able to read this book and predict the company; that is the test of whether it is written well.

### Article 1. "We need AI."

YSWORKS asks: **"What problem are we trying to solve?"** AI is a component, not an outcome (Book XIII, Article 2). Sometimes the honest answer leads to AI; often it leads to a spreadsheet, a policy, or a conversation the business has been avoiding. The client is owed the answer, not the fashion.

### Article 2. "We need automation."

YSWORKS asks: **"What decision should disappear?"** Automation is the removal of a decision from a human day. Named that way, the real questions surface at once: is the decision safe to remove, who supervises its absence, and which decisions should *never* disappear (Book V, Article 2). Automating without naming the decision produces machinery that is busy rather than useful.

### Article 3. "We need a website."

YSWORKS asks: **"What should change after this website exists?"** A website is not a possession; it is an intervention in a system of attention and trust. If nothing measurable is meant to change — more of the right enquiries, fewer of the wrong ones, less explaining of the same ten things — then what is being commissioned is decoration, and the client deserves to know that before paying for it.

### Article 4. "We need software."

YSWORKS asks: **"What system are we actually improving?"** Software is always an amendment to an existing system of people, habits and tools (Book XIII, Article 1). Until that system is named, the software has no definition of success — only a feature list, which is a definition of effort.

### Article 5. "Our competitor just launched something."

YSWORKS asks: **"What do your clients need that they still aren't getting?"** A competitor's launch is information about the competitor. The client's opportunity lives in their own clients' unmet needs, which no amount of imitation can discover. The company does not build responses to other people's press releases.

### Article 6. "Can we skip the analysis? We're in a hurry."

YSWORKS asks: **"Which part of not understanding shall we gamble on?"** Phrased honestly, the request is a bet, and the client is entitled to place it — but knowingly, with the stakes stated (Book III, Article 2). Sometimes a small, reversible bet is exactly right; the company will say so. What it will not do is disguise a gamble as a schedule.

### Article 7. "Just do what the big companies do."

YSWORKS asks: **"They built that for their scale and their problems — what are yours?"** Borrowed architecture is borrowed cost. The practices of a company with a thousand engineers are load-bearing at a thousand engineers; transplanted into a firm of twelve people, they are ritual. The client's size is not a deficiency to disguise; it is a parameter to design for.

### Article 8. "We don't need documentation."

YSWORKS asks: **"Then who is your system's memory?"** There is always documentation; the only choice is its medium. Undocumented systems are documented in one employee's head, on that employee's terms, at that employee's notice period (Book III, Article 4). The company declines to build hostages.

### Article 9. "Make it impressive."

YSWORKS asks: **"Impressive to whom — and what should they do next?"** Impressiveness without a named audience and a next action is self-portraiture. Once the audience is named, *impressive* usually resolves into something plainer and better: clear, fast, honest, exact (Book XIV, Article 2). That is what impresses the only audience that pays.

### Article 10. "It works. Why touch it?"

YSWORKS asks: **"Working as of when?"** *Working* is a snapshot; *maintained* is a state (Book IV, Article 5). The system that works today is ageing against moving dependencies, moving threats and a moving business. The question is not whether to touch it, but whether to touch it deliberately, on a calendar, or accidentally, during an outage.

### Article 11. "We want a chatbot."

YSWORKS asks: **"Which conversation is failing today?"** If the failing conversation can be found — the question asked fifty times, the answer buried in someone's inbox — then something useful can be built, and it may or may not converse. If no failing conversation can be found, the chatbot is a greeter with amnesia, and the company will say so before building it.

### Article 12. "Automate everything."

YSWORKS asks: **"Which decisions do you want to keep?"** The inversion is deliberate. Total automation is not an ambition but an abdication; somewhere in *everything* are the judgements that make the business itself (Book V, Article 2). The valuable exercise is drawing the line — and a client who draws it thoughtfully usually automates more than they expected, and more safely.

### Article 13. "What technologies do you use?"

YSWORKS answers with its own question: **"What problem do you have?"** The company holds tools, and holds them lightly (Book III, Article 3). A firm that leads with its stack is telling the client what shape their problem is allowed to be. The honest order is problem, then system, then tools — and by the third step, the choice is usually obvious enough to be boring.

### Article 14. "Can you do it cheaper?"

YSWORKS answers: **"We can do less. We cannot do worse."** Scope is negotiable; the standard is not (Book XI, Article 1). A smaller system built properly leaves the client with something that works and grows. A full system built badly leaves them with a liability wearing the invoice of an asset. The company will always help shrink a scope honestly — starting with what the problem actually requires.

### Article 15. "We tried technology before. It failed."

YSWORKS asks: **"What did it fail at — the problem, or the understanding of the problem?"** Most failed systems executed faithfully on a misdiagnosis. The failure is evidence, and valuable: it marks where understanding stopped last time. The company begins there, gratefully, rather than pretending the past didn't happen or blaming the previous vendor to flatter itself.

### Article 16. "It must never fail."

YSWORKS answers: **"It will. Let's choose how."** Everything fails; the difference between engineered and unengineered systems is whether failure arrives in a designed direction (Book IV, Article 3). The productive conversation is about which failures are tolerable, which are not, what each nine of reliability costs, and what happens in the first minute after the alarm. A client promised *never* has been sold a story.

### Article 17. "Make us an app."

YSWORKS asks: **"Who opens it on an ordinary Tuesday, and why?"** Not at launch — launches recruit curiosity. On a Tuesday in the third month, with nothing new to see. If there is no honest Tuesday answer, the need is real but the shape is wrong, and something else — often smaller, often invisible — will serve the business better than an icon nobody touches.

### Article 18. "Add AI so we look innovative."

YSWORKS answers: **"Looking innovative is not a property a system can hold."** Systems hold properties like *fast*, *reliable*, *clear* and *honest*; audiences then draw conclusions. AI added for appearance is a cost with a costume (Book V, Article 3) — supervised, maintained, explained, and useless. If innovation is the goal, the company will look for the operational improvement that earns the word.

### Article 19. "Just tell us what to do."

YSWORKS answers: **"We will — and we'll show you why, so you can catch us being wrong."** The company gladly carries the recommendation; it refuses to carry it without the argument (Book VI, Article 2). A client who follows reasoning they can inspect becomes more capable each engagement. A client trained to obey becomes dependent, and dependency is refused as a business model (Book III, Article 6).

### Article 20. The pattern

Read together, the scenarios reduce to one movement, performed twenty ways: **receive a solution, return its problem.** The company does this not to be difficult but because it is the entire difference between selling technology and practising engineering (Book I, Article 4). The first question a client brings is almost always a solution wearing the grammar of a need. The YSWORKS way is to honour the need enough to interrogate the solution — and to do it so consistently that, in time, clients arrive already asking the second question themselves. That is the company working as intended: not accumulating dependents, but propagating a way of thinking. When a client no longer needs Book XVII, Book III, Article 6 has been fulfilled.
